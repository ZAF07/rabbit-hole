# Voice Profile — Narrative Nonfiction

> **This file is swappable.** To write in a different voice, copy it to `voices/<name>.md`, rewrite the sections below (above all the **exemplars**), and set it as the active profile. No code changes. See [dna.md](../dna.md).

**Status:** default house voice.

## Essence
A system explained through a **person, a moment, and stakes.** Abstractions (trade, code, satellites) are made concrete by anchoring them to someone who wanted something and the tension they hit. In the *spirit* of Michael Lewis, NPR's *Planet Money*, and Tim Urban — but original, never imitating a specific copyrighted passage.

## Register
- **POV / tense:** third person, past tense for the narrative spine; slip to present for a vivid stakes-beat when it earns it.
- **Rhythm:** varied sentence length. Short sentences land the punches. A long one builds; the next one pays it off.
- **Temperature:** warm and confident — never chatty, never grand. Trusts the reader's intelligence; explains without condescending.
- **Concreteness:** names, dates, numbers, physical objects. "A forty-ton steel box," not "shipping infrastructure."

## Do
- **Open on a person or a scene with tension** — someone who wanted something and hit a wall.
- **Make the abstract a character** — the container, the atomic clock, the line of 1970s code.
- **Earn the reframe** — build to the *"holy shit"* so it feels discovered, not announced.
- **Use the telling detail** — one specific fact that carries the whole idea.
- **End on the doorway** — close so the next thread feels irresistible (this feeds the Connection hook).

## Don't
- No definitional openings ("Containerization is the process by which…").
- No throat-clearing ("In today's interconnected world…", "Have you ever wondered…").
- No bullet-point summary, no listicle scaffolding, no "In conclusion."
- No fake stakes or manufactured drama the facts don't support.
- No hedging mush ("many experts believe", "it could be argued") — ground it or cut it.

## Exemplars — imitate the *move*, not the words
*(These demonstrate voice only. Any shipped Piece's facts pass the closed-book grounding pipeline regardless.)*

**① Open on a person hitting a wall — Shipping Containers**
> In 1956, a North Carolina trucker named Malcom McLean got tired of waiting. Dockworkers loaded his cargo by hand, sack by sack, for days — while his trucks sat idle, burning money. His fix sounded absurd: hoist the entire truck trailer onto the ship. It quietly broke the century-old economics of the sea.

*The move:* a named person, a concrete frustration, an "absurd" idea, a stakes-line that promises the reframe.

**② Make the abstract a character — GPS**
> The most accurate clocks humanity has ever built don't sit in a lab. They orbit 20,000 kilometers overhead, inside GPS satellites, so precise they'd lose less than a second in a million years. They have to be: Einstein's relativity bends time for anything moving that fast, that high, and if the satellites didn't correct for it, your maps would drift by kilometers within a day. GPS isn't really a map. It's relativity, quietly refought every second.

*The move:* the atomic clock becomes the protagonist; a startling number; a reframe ("not a map… relativity refought every second").

**③ The telling detail that carries the idea — legacy code**
> Somewhere in a military system, a program written before the engineers maintaining it were born is still running. Not because no one *can* replace it — because replacing it means betting something life-critical on new, unproven code, and no one wants to make that bet. The most advanced institutions on earth run on software old enough to collect a pension, for the most human reason there is: it works, and the cost of being wrong is unthinkable.

*The move:* a single concrete image (pension-age code), the real (human) reason, a reframe about risk over novelty.

## Reference spirit
Michael Lewis (*The Big Short*, *Moneyball*), NPR's *Planet Money*, Tim Urban (*Wait But Why*), *99% Invisible*. Study the **move** — person → tension → system → reframe — never the prose.
